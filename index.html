Escape the Turing Trap

A 10-year simulation of how AI deployment shapes the economy — and who gets left behind.

What is this?

This simulation is based on economist Erik Brynjolfsson's concept of the "Turing Trap" — the risk that AI development becomes focused on replacing human labor entirely, rather than making humans more capable.

The trap is subtle: even well-intentioned augmentation can sideline workers if only some people gain access to AI tools. The central question isn't whether AI raises productivity. It's whether humans remain necessary, valuable, and powerful in the system AI creates.

You play as a decision-maker choosing how to deploy AI across 10 years. Your choices shape eight economic metrics — and lead to one of five endings.

Your three strategies
🤖
Replace Workers
Use AI to substitute for human labor. Fast productivity and profit gains — but jobs, wages, and worker leverage collapse. Turing Trap risk climbs quickly.
⚡
Augment Skilled Workers
Give AI tools to already-skilled employees. Productivity rises and some wages grow — but workers without AI access fall behind, creating a two-tier workforce.
🌱
Train & Augment Broadly
Invest in training everyone to use AI. Wages and bargaining power grow broadly — but profits shrink short-term, and jobs still fall as productivity rises. Watch the margin.
The 8 metrics you're tracking
📈
Productivity
Total output per unit of input. Goes up with all three strategies — but high productivity alone doesn't mean workers benefit.
💰
Company Profit
Operating margin retained by the firm. Watch this: if it stays too low for too long, investors can force an automation round on you.
💵
Worker Wages
The share of value flowing to workers as pay. Higher means workers are seen as contributors — not just costs to cut.
👷
Jobs Available
Roles that still require humans. Falls under automation fastest — but even broad training reduces this as workers become more productive per head.
✊
Bargaining Power
Workers' ability to negotiate pay and conditions. Collapses when AI makes them replaceable; grows when their AI skills are genuinely scarce.
⚖️
Inequality
How unevenly income is distributed. Lower is better. High inequality means gains are concentrating at the top while others fall behind.
🔬
New Task Creation
The rate at which AI opens entirely new roles and work categories. Higher means the economy is expanding, not just replacing existing tasks.
🪤
Turing Trap Risk
The risk of AI locking in a world where humans are economically sidelined. Lower is better. Crosses 70 and you fall into the worst ending.
The 5 possible endings
⛓️ The Turing Trap
Trap Risk > 70 or Inequality > 75. Workers lost all leverage. Wealth concentrated in the hands of whoever controlled the AI.
⚖️ Uneven Augmentation
Productivity > 70 and Inequality > 55. New value created — but only for those already positioned to capture it.
🌱 Broad Augmentation
Bargaining Power > 65 and Inequality < 55. The best outcome: productivity rose and workers stayed central to value creation.
🔀 Mixed Transition
No dominant condition met. Productivity grew but the social outcome stayed unstable and contested.
🏦 Corporate Capture
Forced automation triggered twice. Good intentions were overridden by the profit imperative — the board took the wheel.
How to play
  • Each year, pick one of the three strategies. Your choice updates all 8 metrics immediately.
  • Watch the Company Profit bar. If it falls below 35 for two years running, investors force an emergency automation round — you don't get to choose.
  • The color of each metric bar tells you how it's doing: green = healthy, yellow = caution, red = bad.
  • For Inequality and Turing Trap Risk, the goal is to keep the score low — those bars turning red means trouble.
  • There is no single correct strategy. Mixing approaches is often necessary — but mixing poorly can land you in the Mixed Transition ending.
  • The Value Distribution bar at the bottom shows who is actually capturing the gains as the game evolves.
